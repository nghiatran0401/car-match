# CarMatch / SMART Showroom
## Product Requirements Document

CarMatch is a guided automotive retail webapp that helps users move from initial lifestyle and budget inputs to a narrowed vehicle shortlist, side-by-side comparison, vehicle detail review, quote generation, optional offer/booking flow, and showroom/contact discovery.  The product experience should feel like a premium consultative dealership assistant rather than a traditional inventory directory, with a step-by-step flow that reduces decision overload and keeps the buyer focused on the most relevant next action. 

## Product vision

The product should make buying a car feel more like receiving tailored advice than browsing a catalog.  Instead of asking users to search through many models manually, the system should collect a small number of high-signal inputs, interpret the buyer context, produce a curated shortlist, help compare candidates, and guide the user toward a quote, booking, or showroom handoff. 

## Problem statement

Car shoppers often face too many choices too early, which creates confusion and abandonment.  Your diagram reinforces that the product solves this by sequencing the journey into clear stages: gather input, narrow to a shortlist, inspect and compare, configure or quote, then connect to a dealer/showroom. 

## Goals

- Reduce buyer overwhelm through a guided intake and shortlist flow. 
- Generate relevant car matches using user profile, lifestyle, and budget signals. 
- Support informed decision-making through specs and model comparison. 
- Convert interest into measurable lead actions such as quote requests, offers, booking, or showroom visit intent. 
- Preserve a premium dealership tone across all steps. 

## Non-goals

- Real-time lender integration is not required for MVP because the flow can be represented with estimated finance and offer states. 
- Full ERP/DMS integration is not required for MVP unless the implementation team wants to connect real stock and pricing later. 
- User accounts are optional for MVP; the primary goal is guided lead conversion, not account retention. 

## Target users

The primary users are prospective car buyers who want help narrowing down options based on life stage, intended use, and budget.  Secondary users include dealership sales staff or concierge teams who may receive the lead, quote request, booking intent, or showroom visit request generated by the app. 

## Product scope

The product includes six major capability areas visible across the live site and your flow diagram. 

| Capability | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Guided intake | Collect buyer context and filter signals  |
| Shortlist/recommendations | Show the most relevant matching vehicles  |
| Compare/specifications | Help users evaluate differences between candidate models  |
| Vehicle detail/configuration | Present a chosen car in depth with variant and pricing context  |
| Quote/offer/booking | Convert vehicle interest into a commercial next step  |
| Showroom/contact | Help users locate showroom, map, and contact points  |

## User journey

The core end-to-end user flow inferred from the image is:

1. User lands on the homepage and sees a guided question flow. 
2. User enters lifestyle, intended use, and budget-related inputs. 
3. System returns a narrowed shortlist of recommended cars. 
4. User opens one or more candidate vehicles and optionally compares models side by side. 
5. User reviews deeper specifications, benefits, and pricing details. 
6. User can continue toward quote, contract-like commercial details, offer/booking, or financing-related next steps. 
7. User can locate a showroom or contact point on a map to proceed offline. 

## Homepage and intake flow

The initial live site clearly presents the product as a guided intake experience with a hero statement, a 5-question wizard, and lifestyle persona selection as the first step.  The homepage must preserve the visible copy and the consultative framing while serving as the entry point to the larger CarMatch funnel shown in your diagram. 

### Confirmed homepage requirements

- Hero headline: **A smart dealership crafted for you.** 
- Supporting copy: **One guided question at a time, so the buyer can move forward without getting buried in options.** 
- Top navigation: **Profile**, **Recommendations**, **Specifications**, **Quote**. 
- Wizard starts at **QUESTION 1** and shows **0 / 5 ANSWERED**. 
- First question: **Where are you in life?** 
- Answer choices: **First-time buyer**, **Young professional**, **Growing family**, **Established family**, **Executive owner**. 

### Intake objectives

The intake flow should gather only the minimum information needed to rank vehicles well.  Based on the diagram, the inputs should include at least **lifestyle or family status**, **intended use**, and **budget**, because those are explicitly indicated in the left-side journey notes. 

### Intake functional requirements

The system shall present one question at a time in a lightweight wizard.  The system shall support skip and back behavior, because the live app explicitly shows those controls and the first question is marked optional. 

**Acceptance criteria**
- User can complete intake without filling every field. 
- Answered count updates only when an answer is actually selected. 
- Progress reflects the total five-step structure. 
- The app can derive a usable profile from partial answers. 

## Buyer profile generation

The system shall transform questionnaire answers into a buyer profile used by recommendations and downstream messaging.  The profile should express both structured scores and human-readable narrative, such as “young professional prioritizing style and practicality” or “family buyer prioritizing safety and space.” 

### Buyer profile fields

- Persona label
- Life stage
- Intended use
- Budget range
- Priority weights: safety, style, practicality, premium, value, comfort
- Preferred body style or powertrain if captured
- Summary narrative
- Lead quality / intent level

**Acceptance criteria**
- Every completed or partially completed intake generates one profile object.
- The profile is accessible from the Profile section. 
- The recommendation logic consumes the profile without requiring manual re-entry.

## Recommendation shortlist

Your diagram clearly shows a stage where cars are listed after the intake, with a note that the car listing should show the most matched models and the top relevant information for each.  This means the recommendations view must be more than a gallery; it must behave like a curated shortlist surface optimized for quick elimination and confidence-building. 

### Shortlist requirements

Each recommendation card should include:
- Vehicle image
- Brand and model
- Match score or fit indicator
- Key reasons it fits the user
- Top specs at a glance
- Starting price or price band
- CTA to view details
- CTA to compare
- CTA to quote or continue

### Shortlist acceptance criteria

- The shortlist contains only a manageable number of top matches, ideally 3–6 by default. 
- Each card surfaces the most decision-relevant information first. 
- The user can open details for one car from the shortlist. 
- The user can add at least two cars to a compare flow. 

## Compare experience

Your diagram explicitly includes a note about comparing functions between more models.  This makes model comparison a first-class product feature rather than a nice-to-have, so the PRD should define it clearly.

### Compare requirements

The system shall allow users to compare two or more shortlisted models side by side.  The compare view shall emphasize the most decision-critical dimensions, such as price, powertrain, size, performance, safety, comfort, ownership cost, and notable feature differences. 

### Compare acceptance criteria

- User can add vehicles from shortlist or detail page into compare.
- Compare view shows vehicles in side-by-side columns. 
- Each row groups attributes into understandable sections, such as price, performance, dimensions, comfort, and safety. 
- Differences are visually highlighted where values meaningfully differ.
- User can remove a model from compare without leaving the page.

## Vehicle detail page

Your diagram shows a larger dedicated vehicle detail screen with a main image and surrounding data panels, plus a note indicating the user should be able to see detailed data and customizations.  That means the detail page should act as the decision workspace for a selected vehicle, not just a marketing landing page. 

### Detail page requirements

The detail page shall include:
- Vehicle hero image gallery
- Vehicle title, trim, or edition
- Core selling summary
- Price block
- Variant/trim selector if relevant
- Key specs summary
- Finance or payment snapshot if available
- CTA to quote
- CTA to compare
- CTA to booking/offer flow if enabled

### Detail page acceptance criteria

- From shortlist, user can open a dedicated detail page for any recommended vehicle. 
- The page displays both emotional summary and structured specs. 
- The page supports moving into quote or compare from the same context. 
- Any selected trim or option updates the visible price summary.

## Specifications and technical detail

The live site exposes a top-level **Specifications** area, and your diagram shows what looks like a table-like detailed breakdown view.  The specifications module should therefore provide a structured technical reference layer for serious evaluators.

### Specifications requirements

The specifications view shall organize information into categories such as:
- Engine or battery
- Power and torque
- Dimensions
- Seating and cargo
- Fuel economy or range
- Safety systems
- Comfort and infotainment
- Ownership and warranty

### Specifications acceptance criteria

- The user can access specifications from nav and from a selected vehicle. 
- Specs are grouped into readable sections, not one long unstructured list.
- Specs can feed the compare view.
- Missing fields degrade gracefully rather than breaking layout.

## Commercial flow: quote, contract, finance, insurance

Your diagram includes an annotation indicating another flow that may consider sale policy, contract flow, insurance, and bank loan aspects.  That suggests the downstream commercial layer is broader than a simple “contact us” form and should support quote-like and deal-structuring steps, even if only at prototype level. 

### Quote requirements

The quote view shall:
- Preload the selected vehicle
- Show base price and selected trim
- Allow simple adjustments or options
- Capture customer contact details
- Show estimated total or monthly payment if finance mode is enabled
- Allow submission of quote request

### Commercial detail requirements

For a fuller PRD, the product should reserve space for:
- Sale policy or promotional note
- Contract summary or required documents
- Insurance add-on options
- Bank loan or financing estimate
- Reservation or deposit action

### Quote acceptance criteria

- Quote route is accessible from nav and vehicle contexts. 
- Quote view always shows the currently selected vehicle if one exists.
- Submitting quote creates a lead record with selected car and user data.
- Finance estimate recalculates if deposit, term, or rate changes.
- If no vehicle is selected, the user is prompted to choose one.

## Offer and booking flow

The diagram explicitly includes an **Offer / Finalization** stage and notes about booking or lead actions.  This should be formalized as a commercial conversion layer after the user has high purchase intent.

### Offer/booking requirements

The system shall support a post-quote action where the user can:
- Express intent to proceed
- Request consultant follow-up
- Reserve a vehicle or appointment
- Select preferred contact channel
- Optionally choose showroom and preferred date/time

### Offer/booking acceptance criteria

- User can enter a booking or reservation flow after quote.
- User can submit a preferred date/time if appointment scheduling is enabled. 
- The system records booking intent alongside chosen vehicle and customer details.
- Confirmation is shown after submission.

## Showroom and contact point

### Showroom requirements

- Address
- Contact information

- Map with pins

## Recommendation logic

The recommendation engine should combine rule-based filtering with weighted scoring.  The intake flow in the diagram specifically points to using life stage, use case, and budget as core ranking inputs. 

### Matching signals

- Life stage/persona
- Budget fit
- Intended use
- Family size if captured
- Powertrain preference
- Priority weights: safety, style, comfort, practicality, premium, value
- Availability or promotional priority if business wants ranking influence

### Recommendation acceptance criteria

- Different answer profiles produce materially different shortlists.
- Budget mismatch penalizes ranking strongly.
- Recommendation reasons are generated from the strongest matching dimensions.
- The top match is explainable in plain language.

## UX and design principles

The product should maintain the premium, calm, consultative tone from the live homepage throughout the full funnel.  The experience in your diagram also suggests a modular dashboard-like layout for downstream steps, so the UX should balance clarity, sales guidance, and structured information. 

### UX requirements

- One clear primary action per screen
- Guided step progression
- Reassuring microcopy
- Clear state transitions
- Strong visual hierarchy for top recommendation and key specs
- Comparison and pricing data readable at a glance
- Mobile-friendly layouts for shortlist, detail, compare, and quote

### UX acceptance criteria

- A first-time user can complete the intake and reach a shortlist without instructions.
- Compare is understandable without training.
- Quote flow feels like a continuation of the same journey, not a separate system.
- Showroom locator is discoverable from high-intent pages.
